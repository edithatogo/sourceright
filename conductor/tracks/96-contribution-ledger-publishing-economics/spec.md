# Track 96 - Contribution Ledger and Publishing Economics

## Goal

Define a contribution ledger that credits measurable work across the research and publishing lifecycle, including peer review, editorial input, human verification, translation, data curation, code review, reproducibility work, platform development, and commissioning.

## User outcome

Author contributions are no longer the only counted inputs. Reviewers, editors, technical contributors, translators, data curators, software developers, statisticians, reproducibility reviewers, community contributors, and funders can receive transparent, auditable credit.

## Scope

- Extend CRediT-like roles with publishing-process and platform-process roles.
- Record peer review and editorial input as citable or indexable contributions where policy permits.
- Credit human verification of AI-generated outputs.
- Credit translation, accessibility, dissemination, reproducibility, and code-review work.
- Credit platform improvements, plugins, workflows, and modules.
- Define optional commissioning workflows for funders, governments, companies, charities, payers, and health systems.
- Define anti-gaming, conflict, and quality controls.
- Support voluntary profile linkage to ORCID, GitHub, LinkedIn, Google Scholar, ResearchGate, Bluesky, Mastodon, or other public profiles, subject to consent and privacy policy.

## Out of scope

- Default token economics.
- Ranking people by opaque contribution scores.
- Using private profile data without consent.
- Treating quantity of contributions as quality.

## Completion criteria

- Contribution types are documented.
- Profile and identity boundaries are documented.
- Funder and payer commissioning concepts are documented.
- Anti-gaming and transparency requirements are linked to Track 97.
